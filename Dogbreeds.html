    Dog Breeds

    Dog breeds come from all over and have all sorts of shapes and sizes. The visualizations on this page look at the country of origin of many dog breeds, their sizes, and colors. These different graphs show the preferences that people have with dog breeds and where many dog breeds actually originate from.

    This global map traces dog breeds back to their countries of origin, showing concentrations across Europe, Asia, and other continents. Over 40% of the world's dog breeds come from the United Kingdom, France, and Germany, with Great Britain and France each having 57 registered breeds (Link to more info) which can be observed with the small agglomeration of colorful dots in this regions. The visualization demonstrates how different regions developed breeds suited to their specific needs: like German Shepherds bred in Germany in 1899 for herding sheep for example (Example). This global distribution reflects centuries of selective breeding influenced by geography, climate, and cultural needs. The size of each dot represents the highest average longevity for each breed. It is clear that many dog breeds have similar lifespans regardless of their origin country.

    This bar chart ranks countries by the number of dog breeds originally from each country. Germany and England lead significantly, followed by France, Russia, Scotland, China, Mexico, Australia, Switzerland, Ireland, Belgium, and others. The UK has the biggest number of breeds with 29 breeds among the top 101 most popular, followed by Germany with 18 breeds and France with 8 breeds. This visualization supports the other graph shown by giving how many dog breeds are actually from each country. The global distribution of dog breeds is very centralized in Western Europe as shown in both graphs.

    This parallel categories graph reveals patterns between where dog breeds originated and how long they typically live. The flowing bands connect countries like Canada, Germany, England, France, Mexico, Scotland, China, Russia, and Australia to longevity ranges spanning from 8-10 years up to 15-20 years. The visualization shows that breeding traditions and purposes have significantly impacted canine lifespans. Generally, breeds from countries that historically favored smaller companion dogs tend to show longer lifespans (12-20 years), while nations that bred larger working dogs (for herding, guarding, or hunting) often produced breeds with shorter lifespans (8-12 years).

    This detailed pie chart shows the overall breed distribution across all registered dogs in the data, with Pit Bull Terriers, Labrador Retrievers, and Chihuahuas forming the largest segments, followed by numerous other breeds and mixed breeds. The visualization highlights the diversity of the canine population while showing clear favorites. It also reminds people of how many dog breeds exist worldwide and how overwhelming that number can be. It is worth noting that some popular breeds seem to be lower in the chart like Golden Retrievers not even making the ten.

    This vibrant pie chart displays the distribution of fur colors across dog breeds, with combinations like White, Black/Tan, Black/Red, and many multi-color patterns. The chart labeled "Black, Tan, White" suggests a significant portion of dogs have multi-colored coats. The incredible variety of colors reflects both natural genetic variation and centuries of selective breeding for aesthetic preferences. Color patterns often correlate with specific breeds and can sometimes be linked to certain genetic health considerations, making this an interesting dimension for both breed identification and selection.
